# Photosynthesis: How Plants Store Light Energy

## Overview

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy. In plants, most photosynthesis occurs in chloroplasts. Chlorophyll pigments absorb light, especially red and blue wavelengths.

## Overall Equation

The simplified equation is:

6 CO2 + 6 H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6 O2

This equation shows that carbon dioxide and water are used to form glucose and oxygen. It is a summary: the actual process consists of many enzyme-controlled reactions.

## Light-Dependent Reactions

The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes. Light excites electrons in chlorophyll. Water is split, releasing oxygen. The reactions produce ATP and NADPH, which carry energy and reducing power to the Calvin cycle.

## Calvin Cycle

The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma. It does not use light directly. The enzyme RuBisCO helps fix carbon dioxide into organic molecules. ATP and NADPH from the light-dependent reactions help produce G3P, a molecule that can be used to build glucose and other carbohydrates.

## Limiting Factors

The rate of photosynthesis can be limited by light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, temperature, and water availability. Increasing one factor will not keep increasing the rate when another factor becomes limiting.

## Why Photosynthesis Matters

Photosynthesis supplies chemical energy to most ecosystems and releases much of the oxygen used in aerobic respiration. It also removes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere while carbon is incorporated into organic molecules.
